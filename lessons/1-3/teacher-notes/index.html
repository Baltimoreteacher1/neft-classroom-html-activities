Unit 1, Lesson 3: Least Common Multiple6.NS.4

Everything you need to teach this tomorrow. ~45 min.

Objectives

Content: I can find the least common multiple (LCM) of two numbers by listing or comparing their multiples.

Language: I can explain how I found the LCM using the words multiple, common multiple, skip counting, and least common multiple.

Before class

  • Print / open: guided notes, homework, slides.
  • Have the family page link ready for absent students.
  • Preview the key vocabulary below with ESOL students.

Warm-up

Let's find the LCM of 3 and 5. What are the multiples of 3? (3, 6, 9, 12, 15.)

Teaching notes

Key idea: The LCM is the FIRST number that shows up in both numbers' multiple lists.

I do:

  1. I want the LCM of 4 and 6. I skip count the multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24.
  2. Then I skip count the multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24.
  3. I look for the first number in BOTH lists. 12 is in both lists, and it comes before 24.
  4. So the LCM of 4 and 6 is 12. I check: 12 ÷ 4 = 3 and 12 ÷ 6 = 2, so both go into 12 evenly.

You do: Next, you will sort multiples to find the common multiples of two numbers and pick the smallest one. Remember: the LCM is the SMALLEST multiple the two numbers share.

Common misconception

A common mistake in Least Common Multiple is skipping the key idea: "The LCM is the FIRST number that shows up in both numbers' multiple lists." — always check your work against this rule before you submit.

ESOL / SPED supports

  • Pre-teach: Multiple, Least common multiple, Common multiple, Skip counting, Prime factorization (Spanish + visuals are in the lesson vocabulary).
  • Offer the sentence frames: “I know ___ because ___.” “First, I ___. Then, I ___.” “The answer is ___, so ___.”
  • Chunk the I-do steps; let students restate each step to a partner before moving on.
  • Provide the worked example as a reference while they practice.

Exit ticket

What is the LCM of 9 and 12?

  • 36 ✓ correct
  • 108
  • 3
  • 72

Multiples of 9: 9, 18, 27, 36, ... Multiples of 12: 12, 24, 36, ... The smallest common multiple is 36.

Reteach

Re-run the I-do example with smaller numbers and have students narrate each step using the key idea.
