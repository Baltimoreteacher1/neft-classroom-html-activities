Unit 3, Lesson 5: Compare Ratios6.RP.3

A quick guide for families and for any student who missed class.

What students are learning

To compare ratios fairly, make one amount the same in both, or find the unit rate (the amount for just 1). Then you can see which ratio is bigger.

Key idea: Compare ratios fairly by making one part equal or by finding the unit rate.

Key words

  • Unit rate — A rate for just 1 of something, like cost for 1 item.
    Español: Tasa unitaria — Una tasa para solo 1 de algo, como el precio de 1 artículo.
  • Equivalent ratio — Two ratios that mean the same thing.
    Español: Razón equivalente — Dos razones que significan lo mismo.
  • Compare — To look at ratios and see which is bigger, smaller, or equal.
    Español: Comparar — Mirar razones para ver cuál es mayor, menor o igual.
  • Simplify — To make a ratio as small as possible while keeping the same comparison.
    Español: Simplificar — Hacer una razón lo más pequeña posible sin cambiar la comparación.
  • Common denominator — A bottom number that two fractions can share so you can compare them.
    Español: Denominador común — Un número de abajo que dos fracciones pueden compartir para compararlas.

Example

  1. Chef Reyes uses 3 tbsp cocoa for 5 oz milk (3:5). Chef Tran uses 4 tbsp for 7 oz milk (4:7).
  2. I cannot compare yet because the milk amounts are different.
  3. I scale both to 35 oz of milk. For Reyes: 5 x 7 = 35, so 3 x 7 = 21 tbsp, giving 21:35.
  4. For Tran: 7 x 5 = 35, so 4 x 5 = 20 tbsp, giving 20:35.
  5. Now the milk is equal, so I compare cocoa: 21 > 20, so Chef Reyes's cocoa is more chocolatey.

Practice at home

  1. A bakery sells 4 muffins for $10 and another bakery sells 6 muffins for $14. Which bakery has the lower price per muffin?

Answer check

  1. A bakery sells 4 muffins for $10 and another bakery sells 6 muffins for $14. Which bakery has the lower price per muffin? → The second bakery

Common mistake

A common mistake in Compare Ratios is skipping the key idea: "Compare ratios fairly by making one part equal or by finding the unit rate." — always check your work against this rule before you submit.

How families can help at home

  • Ask your student to teach you the example above in their own words.
  • Have them say why each step works, not just the answer.
  • Use the sentence starters below so they explain their thinking in full sentences.
