Unit 1, Lesson 1: Prime Factorization6.NS.4

Everything you need to teach this tomorrow. ~50 min.

Objectives

Content: I can write a number as a product of its prime factors using a factor tree.

Language: I can explain how I broke a number down using the words prime number, composite number, factor, and exponent.

Before class

  • Print / open: guided notes, homework, slides.
  • Have the family page link ready for absent students.
  • Preview the key vocabulary below with ESOL students.

Warm-up

Let's factor 12 together. What is one way to split 12 into two factors? (You might say 3 × 4.)

Teaching notes

Key idea: Keep breaking a number apart until every factor is a prime number you cannot split anymore.

I do:

  1. I want the prime factorization of 60. I start by splitting it: 60 = 6 × 10.
  2. 6 is not prime, so I break it: 6 = 2 × 3. Both 2 and 3 are prime, so I stop those branches.
  3. 10 is not prime, so I break it: 10 = 2 × 5. Both 2 and 5 are prime, so I stop.
  4. Now every factor is prime: 60 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 5.
  5. Using exponents, I write it as 2² × 3 × 5. I check: 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 = 60. Correct.

You do: Next, you will sort numbers into prime and composite, then build factor trees on your own. Remember: keep splitting until every branch is a prime number.

Common misconception

A common mistake in Prime Factorization is skipping the key idea: "Keep breaking a number apart until every factor is a prime number you cannot split anymore." — always check your work against this rule before you submit.

ESOL / SPED supports

  • Pre-teach: Prime number, Composite number, Prime factorization, Factor tree, Exponent (Spanish + visuals are in the lesson vocabulary).
  • Offer the sentence frames: “I know ___ because ___.” “First, I ___. Then, I ___.” “The answer is ___, so ___.”
  • Chunk the I-do steps; let students restate each step to a partner before moving on.
  • Provide the worked example as a reference while they practice.

Exit ticket

What is the prime factorization of 40?

  • 2 × 2 × 2 × 5 ✓ correct
  • 4 × 10
  • 5 × 8
  • 2 × 20

40 = 2 × 20 = 2 × 2 × 10 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 5. All factors (2, 2, 2, 5) are prime.

Reteach

Re-run the I-do example with smaller numbers and have students narrate each step using the key idea.

Extension

A party planner has 72 balloons and wants to know every possible way to arrange them in equal rows. She uses prime factorization to find all the factors of 72.
