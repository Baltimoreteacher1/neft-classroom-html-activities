Warm-up

A booth charges $4 for 8 games. What do we divide?

Teaching notes

Key idea: To find a unit rate, divide the total cost by the number of items. The lower cost per item is the better buy.

I do:

  1. Booth A charges $3 for 5 games. I want the cost for just 1 game.
  2. I divide the cost by the games: $3 ÷ 5 = $0.60.
  3. So Booth A costs $0.60 per game. That is the unit rate.
  4. Now I can compare this booth fairly with any other booth.

You do: In the table, find the cost per game for each booth by dividing. Then decide which booth has the lowest unit rate — that is the better buy.

Common misconception

A common mistake in Rates and Unit Rates is skipping the key idea: "To find a unit rate, divide the total cost by the number of items. The lower cost per item is the better buy." — always check your work against this rule before you submit.

ESOL / SPED supports

  • Pre-teach: Rate, Unit Rate, Per, Ratio, Better Buy, Divide (Spanish + visuals are in the lesson vocabulary).
  • Offer the sentence frames: “I know ___ because ___.” “First, I ___. Then, I ___.” “The answer is ___, so ___.”
  • Chunk the I-do steps; let students restate each step to a partner before moving on.
  • Provide the worked example as a reference while they practice.

Exit ticket

A store sells 6 pencils for $1.50 and 10 pencils for $2.80. Which is the better buy?

  • 6 for $1.50 — $0.25 each ✓ correct
  • 10 for $2.80 — $0.28 each
  • They cost the same per pencil
  • Not enough information

$1.50 ÷ 6 = $0.25 per pencil. $2.80 ÷ 10 = $0.28 per pencil. $0.25 < $0.28, so 6 for $1.50 is the better buy.

Reteach

Re-run the I-do example with smaller numbers and have students narrate each step using the key idea.
