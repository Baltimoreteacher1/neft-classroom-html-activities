Unit 4, Lesson 1: Rates and Unit Rates6.RP.2

A quick guide for families and for any student who missed class.

What students are learning

A rate compares two amounts with different units. A unit rate tells you the amount for just 1 of something, like the cost for 1 game.

Key idea: To find a unit rate, divide the total cost by the number of items. The lower cost per item is the better buy.

Key words

  • Rate — A ratio comparing two amounts with different units, like miles per hour.
    Español: Tasa — Una razón que compara dos cantidades con unidades distintas, como millas por hora.
  • Unit Rate — A rate for just 1 of something, like cost for 1 item.
    Español: Tasa unitaria — Una tasa para solo 1 de algo, como el precio de 1 artículo.
  • Per — For each one. Example: 5 dollars per book.
    Español: Por — Por cada uno. Ejemplo: 5 dólares por libro.
  • Ratio — A way to compare two amounts.
    Español: Razón — Una manera de comparar dos cantidades.
  • Better Buy — The choice that costs less for each item.
    Español: Mejor compra — La opción que cuesta menos por cada artículo.
  • Divide — Splitting a total into equal groups.
    Español: Dividir — Repartir un total en grupos iguales.

Example

  1. Booth A charges $3 for 5 games. I want the cost for just 1 game.
  2. I divide the cost by the games: $3 ÷ 5 = $0.60.
  3. So Booth A costs $0.60 per game. That is the unit rate.
  4. Now I can compare this booth fairly with any other booth.

Practice at home

  1. A store sells 6 pencils for $1.50 and 10 pencils for $2.80. Which is the better buy?

Answer check

  1. A store sells 6 pencils for $1.50 and 10 pencils for $2.80. Which is the better buy? → 6 for $1.50 — $0.25 each

Common mistake

A common mistake in Rates and Unit Rates is skipping the key idea: "To find a unit rate, divide the total cost by the number of items. The lower cost per item is the better buy." — always check your work against this rule before you submit.

How families can help at home

  • Ask your student to teach you the example above in their own words.
  • Have them say why each step works, not just the answer.
  • Use the sentence starters below so they explain their thinking in full sentences.
