Unit 9, Lesson 3: Compare and Order Integers6.NS.7

A quick guide for families and for any student who missed class.

What students are learning

To compare integers means to see which one is greater or less. On a number line, numbers get bigger as you go right and smaller as you go left.

Key idea: The number farther to the left is always less; the number farther to the right is always greater.

Key words

  • Compare — To see if a number is bigger, smaller, or equal to another.
    Español: Comparar — Ver si un número es mayor, menor o igual que otro.
  • Order — To put numbers in order, smallest to biggest or biggest to smallest.
    Español: Ordenar — Poner números en orden, de menor a mayor o de mayor a menor.
  • Greater than — Farther right on a number line. The > sign.
    Español: Mayor que — Más a la derecha en la recta numérica. El signo >.
  • Less than — Farther left on a number line. The < sign.
    Español: Menor que — Más a la izquierda en la recta numérica. El signo <.
  • Negative integer — A whole number smaller than zero, like -5.
    Español: Entero negativo — Un número entero menor que cero, como -5.
  • Integer — Whole numbers and their opposites, like -2, -1, 0, 1, 2.
    Español: Número entero — Números enteros y sus opuestos, como -2, -1, 0, 1, 2.

Example

  1. I will compare -7 and -5. They are both negative, so I look at the number line.
  2. -7 is 7 steps left of zero. -5 is only 5 steps left of zero.
  3. -7 is farther to the left, so -7 is the smaller number.
  4. I write -7 < -5. The open side of the symbol points to the bigger number, -5.

Practice at home

  1. Which list shows the integers in order from LEAST to GREATEST?

Answer check

  1. Which list shows the integers in order from LEAST to GREATEST? → -5, -2, 0, 3, 7

Common mistake

A common mistake in Compare and Order Integers is skipping the key idea: "The number farther to the left is always less; the number farther to the right is always greater." — always check your work against this rule before you submit.

How families can help at home

  • Ask your student to teach you the example above in their own words.
  • Have them say why each step works, not just the answer.
  • Use the sentence starters below so they explain their thinking in full sentences.
