Unit 9 Projects — Teacher Answer Key

Two-Variable Relationships  |  6.EE.9  |  y = kx, tables, graphs

For teachers only — this page shows fully worked solutions using each project's "Use example numbers" values. Students enter their own real numbers (a channel they would run / their family's actual plan + a real competitor), so totals vary, but the reasoning is identical. Use these as your grading reference and model responses.

Standards: 6.EE.9 — identify independent and dependent variables, write equations y = kx, analyze relationships using tables and graphs.

Project arc: Each version follows Self → Peer → World. Self = the student's own real context; Peer = a classmate interview auto-compare; World = built-in real benchmarks + a What-If Lab. A show-your-work grid (find y at x=3, find x at a target, 12-period total) is tolerance-checked against the student's own k. Level 1 prefills a friendly k; Level 2 adds non-proportional y = kx + b reasoning.

Version A — Streaming Channel Growth Lab

Part 1 · Self Design YOUR Channel

Variables · Equation · Table · Graph · 6.EE.9
Topic = skateboarding tricks k = 12 subscribers/week Starting subscribers = 0
y = 12x
Independent variable (x): weeks — the student controls how many weeks have passed.
Dependent variable (y): subscribers — the total depends on how many weeks have passed.
k = 12 is the constant of proportionality (a unit rate: 12 subs per week). After x weeks, y = 12 × x. Students must justify their chosen k with research ("why this k?").
Data table (y = 12x), weeks 0–5
Weeks (x) Equation Subscribers (y)
0 y = 12 × 0 0
1 y = 12 × 1 12
2 y = 12 × 2 24
3 y = 12 × 3 36
4 y = 12 × 4 48
5 y = 12 × 5 60
Each y increases by 12 (constant rate of change). Each row is an ordered pair (x, y) plottable on the Unit 7 coordinate plane.
Graph readings (k = 12)
Find y: y = 12 × 7 = 84 subscribers at week 7.
Find x: x = 300 ÷ 12 = 25.00 weeks to reach 300 subscribers.
The SVG line passes through the origin (0, 0) and rises 12 per week.
Show Your Work grid (checked vs. student's own k = 12)
1. y at x = 3: 12 × 3 = 36 (tolerance 0)
2. x at target y = 240: 240 ÷ 12 = 20 weeks (tolerance ±0.05)
3. 12-month total (52 weeks): 12 × 52 = 624 subscribers (tolerance 0)

3 (Proficient): Equation and table correct; at least one graph reading and most work-grid answers correct.
2 (Developing): One variable correct or some work attempted with computation errors.

Part 3 · World Creator Benchmarks + What-If Lab

Compare to Real Data · 6.EE.9
My k = 12 Benchmark sample: 5, 25, 120, 600, 1500, 4000
My k vs. benchmark average
Benchmark average = (5 + 25 + 120 + 600 + 1500 + 4000) ÷ 6 = 6250 ÷ 6 ≈ 1041.67 subs/week.
A starting channel's k = 12 is far below the all-tier average (the average is pulled up by large channels) but is realistic for the "growing channel" tier. The meter shows the student's k below the sample average — accept any reasoning that references the benchmark sample.
What-If Lab: with k = 12 at week 10, before = 120 subscribers. Adding a 500-subscriber viral week → after = 620. Emphasize that y = 12x + 500 is no longer proportional (does not pass through the origin).
Level 2 — y = kx + 200 (one-time boost)
y = kx + 200 is NOT proportional — it crosses the y-axis at 200, not the origin. With the same k, your line and the friend's line are parallel, so they are never equal: the friend stays exactly 200 ahead forever.
Sample 4 / 3 / 2 Rubric Scoring — World
4 (Expert): Compares k to the benchmark sample with reasoning; What-If and Level 2 correctly note loss of proportionality.
3 (Proficient): Reasonable comparison to benchmark data.
2 (Developing): Comparison attempted.

Deliverable Growth Report — Sample Expert Response

"My channel is about skateboarding tricks. My independent variable is weeks (x) and my dependent variable is subscribers (y). My equation is y = 12x — I chose k = 12 because real skating channels that post twice a week gain about 12 subscribers a week. At week 7 I will have 84 subscribers, and I will hit 240 subscribers at week 20. My channel grows faster than my classmate's (k = 8) because my k is bigger, so my line is steeper and I lead by 40 subscribers at week 10."

Independent variable (x): months — chosen by the family.
Dependent variable (y): total cost ($) — depends on months paid.

Part 3 · World Real Carrier Prices + What-If Lab

Compare to Real Data · 6.EE.9
My Plan A k = $25/month Carrier sample: 15, 25, 30, 40, 50, 51, 65
My plan vs. carrier average
Carrier average = (15 + 25 + 30 + 40 + 50 + 51 + 65) ÷ 7 = 276 ÷ 7 ≈ $39.43/month.
A $25 plan is below the average — a good deal, comparable to Visible. Accept any reasoning that cites the carrier sample.
What-If Lab: Plan A at month 12 = $300. Adding a one-time $35 sign-up fee → $335. y = 25x + 35 is no longer proportional (y-intercept is 35, not 0).
Level 2 — find when two lines are equal (y = kx + 40)
y = kx + 40 is NOT proportional (it starts at $40, not the origin). To find when a flat-rate Plan A (e.g., y = 25x) equals a cheaper-but-fee plan (e.g., y = 18x + 40), set them equal: 25x = 18x + 40 → 7x = 40 → x ≈ 5.7 months. Before ~5.7 months the fee plan is pricier; after, it is cheaper.
Sample 4 / 3 / 2 Rubric Scoring — World
4 (Expert): Compares to the carrier sample; What-If and Level 2 correctly identify loss of proportionality and solve the equal-cost month.
3 (Proficient): Reasonable comparison to real prices.
2 (Developing): Comparison attempted.

Deliverable Recommendation — Sample Expert Response

"The independent variable is months (x) and the dependent variable is total cost (y). Plan A (our T-Mobile plan) is y = 25x and Plan B (Mint Mobile, which I looked up) is y = 18x. After 6 months Plan A costs $150 and Plan B costs $108, and over 12 months Plan A is $300 versus Plan B's $216. I recommend Plan B because its lower monthly rate k = 18 makes a flatter line and saves my family $84 a year — as long as there is no big sign-up fee that would break the proportional relationship."
