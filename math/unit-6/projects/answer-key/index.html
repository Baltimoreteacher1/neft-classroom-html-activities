Worked solutions for both project versions using the "Use example numbers" values. Covers the Self → Peer → World phases, Level 1 / Level 2 differentiation, the equivalent-expression prover, sample expert responses, and rubric scoring notes.

🔒 For teachers — these projects are personalized. Students design their own game (Version A) or model an app they actually use (Version B), so their base, exponent, coin rate r, flat bonus k, monthly price p, setup fee, and months n will differ. The worked solutions below use the "Use example numbers" values; grade the method (correct expression, correct order of operations, sound reasoning), not a single numeric answer. Both versions follow a Self → Peer → World arc with Level 1 and Level 2 differentiation.

Game Studio Scoring Engine — Worked Solutions

Phase 1  ·  Powers & Exponents  ·  6.EE.1

Level-Up Score (student picks base & exponent)

Example base = 3 Example level (exponent) = 4 Level 1 default: 2³ = 8
Formula: score = baselevel = base × base × … (exponent times)
Expanded form: 3 × 3 × 3 × 3
Step-by-step: 3 × 3 = 9 → 9 × 3 = 27 → 27 × 3 = 81
34 = 81 points
81 points

The expanded form shows 4 factors of 3 — students should count them. A common error is computing 3 × 4 = 12 instead of 34 = 81. The exponent counts how many times the base appears as a factor, not how many times to multiply by the exponent itself.

Phase 2 (Part 1 · Self)  ·  Write & Evaluate Their Own Coin Bonus  ·  6.EE.2

Coin Bonus Expression: rc + k (student picks r, k, c)

Example r = 3 Example k = 50 Example c = 12
Student's expression: r·c + k  |  Example r=3, k=50, c=12
3 × 12 + 50
= 36 + 50
= 86 points
86 points (with example numbers)
Method to grade: the value should equal (their r) × (their c) + (their k). Coefficient = r (rate per coin), variable = c (coins), constant = k (flat bonus).
Level 1 (multiple choice): the correct option for "3 points each coin plus a 50 bonus" is 3c + 50 (option B).
Level 2 (name the parts): a correct response names their own coefficient, variable c, and constant — e.g., "coefficient 3, variable c, constant 50."

Common errors: multiplying base × exponent in Phase 1, or writing "r + c + k" (dropping the coefficient as a multiplier) or "rc × k" (multiplying the bonus instead of adding it). The setup is now personalized, so confirm the arithmetic matches the student's own r, k, and c rather than a fixed key.

Phase 3 (Part 2 · Peer)  ·  Trade Formulas & Find the Crossover  ·  6.EE.2c

Trade Formulas & Compare

Mine (example): 3c + 50 Partner (example): 5c + 20 Coins c = 12
Evaluate both formulas at the same coin count c = 12
Mine: 3 × 12 + 50 = 36 + 50 = 86
Partner: 5 × 12 + 20 = 60 + 20 = 80
At c = 12, my formula rewards more (86 > 80).
Mine: 86  |  Partner: 80

Crossover = where rc + k = r₂c + k₂. With the example numbers: 3c + 50 = 5c + 20 → 30 = 2c → c = 15. For c < 15 the higher-bonus formula leads; for c > 15 the higher-rate formula pulls ahead. The page computes the crossover live; grade whether the student identifies who rewards more at their chosen c and explains the switch point (Level 2 solves it algebraically).

Phase 4 (Part 3 · World)  ·  Compare to Real Games & Prove Equivalence  ·  6.EE.3 · 6.EE.4

World Meter & Equivalent-Expression Prover

Sample: 8 game scoring formulas rc + k Prover: 2(c + 3) vs 2c + 6
World meter compares the student's score to the sample at the same test coins.
Equivalent-expression prover tests c = 4, 7, 10:
c = 4: 2(7) = 14 and 2(4) + 6 = 14 ✓
c = 7: 2(10) = 20 and 2(7) + 6 = 20 ✓
c = 10: 2(13) = 26 and 2(10) + 6 = 26 ✓
2(c + 3) ≡ 2c + 6 — equivalent ✓

They match at every value because the distributive property gives 2(c + 3) = 2·c + 2·3 = 2c + 6 (6.EE.3 → 6.EE.4). Matching at a few values is strong evidence; the distributive expansion is the proof. The What-If lab adds one more coin and shows the score rises by exactly the rate r.

Level 2 — design a third, fairer formula: any reasonable rc + k with justification earns credit, e.g., "4c + 35 sits between the two so neither coin-heavy nor bonus-heavy players are punished."
Sample Expert Response — Design Document (4 / Expert) In my game Quest Blaster, the level-up score at base 3 raised to level 4 is 81 points (3 × 3 × 3 × 3). My coin bonus expression is 3c + 50 — coefficient 3, variable c, constant 50 — which gives 86 points for 12 coins (3 × 12 + 50). I traded with my partner whose formula is 5c + 20; at 12 coins mine rewards more (86 vs 80), but the formulas cross over at c = 15, after which my partner's higher rate wins. Compared to the sample games my scoring is mid-range, and I proved 2(c + 3) = 2c + 6 by the distributive property, testing c = 4, 7, 10. I recommend keeping a higher flat bonus because most players in my game collect fewer than 15 coins per level.
Sample 4 / 3 / 2 Rubric Scoring Note 4 — Expert: Expanded form shown for the exponent; own coin expression written, evaluated, and parts named; peer comparison with the crossover identified and justified; world comparison with reasoning; equivalence proved and explained via the distributive property; design doc uses every computed number.
3 — Proficient: All calculations correct; peer and world comparisons present; design doc uses most numbers.
2 — Developing: Exponent treated as multiplication (3 × 4 = 12), expression missing a coefficient or constant, or comparison attempted without identifying who rewards more.

App Pricing Engine — Worked Solutions

Phase 1 (Part 1 · Self)  ·  Write & Evaluate Their Own Cost Expression  ·  6.EE.2 · 6.EE.3

Cost Expression: pn + fee (student picks p, fee, n)

Example p = 9.99 Example fee = 4.50 Example n = 6
Order of operations: multiply p × n first, then add the one-time fee.
Step 1 — multiply: 9.99 × 6 = $59.94
Step 2 — add setup fee: $59.94 + $4.50 = $64.44
$64.44 (with example numbers)

Method to grade: total should equal (their p) × (their n) + (their fee). Coefficient = p (monthly price), variable = n (months), constant = fee.
Level 1 (multiple choice): "$9.99 each month plus a one-time $4.50 fee" is 9.99n + 4.50 (option B). Level 2 (name the parts): names their own coefficient, variable n, and constant. The classic error is adding first: 9.99 × (6 + 4.50) = $104.90 instead of multiplying first.

Phase 2 (Part 2 · Peer)  ·  Trade Plans & Find the Crossover  ·  6.EE.2c

Trade Plans & Compare

Mine (example): 9.99n + 4.50 Partner (example): 7.50n + 12.00 Months n = 6
Evaluate both plans at the same number of months n = 6
Mine: 9.99 × 6 + 4.50 = 59.94 + 4.50 = $64.44
Partner: 7.50 × 6 + 12.00 = 45.00 + 12.00 = $57.00
At n = 6 the partner's plan is cheaper by $7.44.
Mine: $64.44  |  Partner: $57.00

Crossover = where pn + fee = p₂n + fee₂. With the example numbers: 9.99n + 4.50 = 7.50n + 12.00 → 2.49n = 7.50 → n ≈ 3.01 months. For n ≤ 3 the lower-fee plan is cheaper; for n ≥ 4 the lower monthly price wins. The page computes the crossover live; Level 2 asks students to solve it algebraically.

Distributive Property Reference (Level 2)  ·  6.EE.3

Justify Equivalence & Design a Fairer Plan

Distributive property worked example: a(b + c) = a·b + a·c
3(8 + 4): inside first → 3 × 12 = 36; distribute → 3·8 + 3·4 = 24 + 12 = 36. Both give 36.
Level 2 — justify in writing: a full-credit response states 2(n + 3) = 2·n + 2·3 = 2n + 6 and explains that distributing the 2 to each term inside is why the two forms are equivalent for every n.
Level 2 — design a third, fairer plan: any reasonable pn + fee with justification, e.g., "8.99n + 6.00 keeps a moderate monthly price and a small fee so short- and long-term users both pay a fair amount."
Sample Expert Response — Pricing Memo (4 / Expert) Manager, the app I use is StreamPro at $9.99 per month plus a one-time $4.50 setup fee, so its expression is 9.99n + 4.50 — coefficient 9.99, variable n, constant 4.50. After 6 months the total is $64.44 (9.99 × 6 + 4.50). My partner's plan is 7.50n + 12.00, which costs $57.00 at 6 months; the two plans cross over at about n = 3, so for short subscriptions StreamPro wins and for longer ones the partner's plan wins. I verified that 2(n + 3) = 2n + 6 using the distributive property, testing n = 4, 7, 10. I recommend the lower-monthly-price plan for anyone subscribing four or more months.

3 — Proficient: All calculations correct; peer and world comparisons present; memo uses most values.
2 — Developing: Cost expression evaluated with the wrong operation order (adding before multiplying), distribution error (missing one term), or comparison attempted without identifying which plan costs less.
