Integers & Coordinate Plane · Grade 6 · Standards 6.NS.5, 6.NS.6, 6.NS.7, 6.NS.8

For teachers only — do not distribute to students. These projects now follow a Self → Peer → World arc: students enter their own real numbers (places, depths, temperatures), so answers will vary from student to student. What stays constant is the reasoning and the procedure — the worked examples below use the "Use example numbers" fallback values so you have a parallel model. Grade the method (correct quadrant signs, |n| as distance from 0, correct ordering, valid distance reasoning), not a single right answer. Version A

Theme Park Map Designer

Standards: 6.NS.6 (ordered pairs, reflections) · 6.NS.8 (distance on the coordinate plane)

Phase 1 — Map the Places You Care About (Self) 6.NS.6 · Ordered Pairs & Quadrants

"Use example numbers" fallback (students substitute their own real places):

Home (x, y) = (0, 0)
School (x, y) = (5, −3)
Park (x, y) = (−7, 4)
Home (0, 0): x = 0 and y = 0 → the origin → on the axes, not in any quadrant (the student's chosen reference point).
School (5, −3): x = 5 (positive), y = −3 (negative) → sign pattern (+, −) → Quadrant IV
Park (−7, 4): x = −7 (negative), y = 4 (positive) → sign pattern (−, +) → Quadrant II
Quadrants reached: this map reaches 2 quadrants (II and IV).

Key rule: Quadrant I (+,+) · Quadrant II (−,+) · Quadrant III (−,−) · Quadrant IV (+,−). Any point with a 0 coordinate lies on an axis, not in a quadrant. Answers vary — grade the sign reasoning, not the specific coordinates.

Example: Home → origin  |  School → QIV  |  Park → QII

Phase 2 — Mirror One of Your Places (Self) 6.NS.6b · Reflections Across Axes

"Use example numbers" fallback:

Original point (x, y) = (3, 4)
Reflect across x-axis
Rule — reflecting across the x-axis: keep x the same; negate y.
Original: (3, 4)
New x = 3 (unchanged)
New y = −4 (negate 4)
Reflected point: (3, −4)

Why y changes: The x-axis is horizontal. Reflecting flips the point to the opposite side vertically — the horizontal distance (x) stays the same, but the vertical position (y) reverses sign.

Compare to other axes:
  Reflect (3, 4) across the y-axis: negate x, keep y → (−3, 4)
  Reflect (3, 4) across both axes: negate both → (−3, −4)
Reflected point: (3, −4)

Phase 3 — Measure a Walking Distance (Self) 6.NS.8 · Distance on the Coordinate Plane

"Use example numbers" fallback:

Point A = (2, 3)
Point B = (2, −5)
Shared coordinate check: Both points have x = 2 (same) and y-values 3 and −5 (different). Because they share x = 2, they lie on a vertical line; the distance is the absolute difference of their y-values.

Formula: distance = |y₂ − y₁| = |−5 − 3| = |−8| = 8 units

Why absolute value? Distance is always positive. Without absolute value we might subtract in the "wrong order" and get a negative result — |−8| = 8 eliminates that possibility.
Distance = 8 units

Phase 4 — Quick Checks 6.NS.6 · 6.NS.8

Quick Check 1: What is the distance between (2, 3) and (2, 9)?
Both points share x = 2 (vertical line).
Distance = |y₂ − y₁| = |9 − 3| = |6| = 6 units
Correct answer: 6

JavaScript check: if (v === 6)

Quick Check 2: What are the coordinates of (4, −5) reflected across the x-axis?
Reflect across x-axis: keep x the same, negate y.
New x = 4 (unchanged)
New y = −(−5) = 5
Reflected point: (4, 5)
Correct answer: x = 4, y = 5

JavaScript check: if (x === 4 && y === 5)

Part 2 — Peer Compare (worked example) 6.NS.6 · 6.NS.8 · Compare & reason

Example: using the fallback map (reaches 2 quadrants; walking distance 8 units), suppose a partner reports a map reaching 4 quadrants and two places at (−3, 5) and (4, 5).

Quadrants reached: me = 2, partner = 4 → difference = |4 − 2| = 2. The partner's map spans more of the plane.

Partner walking distance: (−3, 5) and (4, 5) share y = 5, so distance = |4 − (−3)| = |7| = 7 units.
Compare distances: me = 8, partner = 7 → difference = |8 − 7| = 1 unit. My route is 1 unit longer.

The auto-compare cards show me, partner, and the absolute difference for each row.
Quadrants 2 vs 4 (diff 2)  |  Distance 8 vs 7 (diff 1)

Sample Expert Response (4/4 Deliverable)

Sample Park Blueprint using the "example numbers" map (student work will use their own real places):
"My map's origin (0, 0) is my Home. My School is at (5, −3) in Quadrant IV because x is positive and y is negative, and the Park is at (−7, 4) in Quadrant II because x is negative and y is positive — so my map reaches 2 quadrants. I reflected the point (3, 4) across the x-axis to get (3, −4); the x-coordinate stayed the same while the y-coordinate changed sign because reflecting across the x-axis flips the vertical position. The walking distance from A(2, 3) to B(2, −5) is 8 units because both points share x = 2 and |−5 − 3| = |−8| = 8."
Sample 4 / 3 / 2 Rubric Scoring Guide:
4 — Expert: All three quadrants correctly identified with sign reasoning explicitly stated ("x is negative, y is positive"); reflection explanation states which value changed and why; distance calculation shows the absolute-value work; blueprint uses real coordinates throughout.
3 — Proficient: All quadrants correct; correct reflection and distance; blueprint uses most coordinates but may skip reasoning steps.
2 — Developing: One or two quadrant errors; one coordinate of the reflection correct; distance attempted but has a computation error or skips absolute value.
Version B

Submarine Mission Control

Standards: 6.NS.5 (integers in context) · 6.NS.7 (absolute value, compare & order integers)

Phase 1 — Your Highest & Lowest Places (Self) 6.NS.5 · Integers in Context

"Use example numbers" fallback (students substitute their own real highs/lows):

Highest place = +2500 m
Lowest place = −85 m
Highest (+2500 m): Positive because it is above sea level (the zero reference). +2500 means 2500 m above sea level.

Lowest (−85 m): Negative because it is below sea level. −85 means 85 m below sea level.

Total vertical span:
  |−85| + 2500 = 85 + 2500 = 2585 meters
(distance below sea level + distance above sea level = total span)
High: 2500 m above  |  Low: 85 m below  |  Total span: 2585 m

Phase 2 — Distance from Sea Level (Self · Absolute Value) 6.NS.7c · Absolute Value

"Use example numbers" fallback:

Depth reading = −120 m
Absolute value definition: |n| = distance from 0 on the number line; always ≥ 0.

|−120| = 120
The submarine is 120 meters below sea level.

Why absolute value? −120 is 120 units to the left of 0. The sign tells direction (below); absolute value gives only the magnitude (distance). Removing the negative sign gives 120.
|−120| = 120 meters from sea level

Phase 3 — Order Your Temperature Life (Self) 6.NS.7 · Compare & Order Integers

"Use example numbers" fallback (students use their own real temperatures):

Today = 18 °C
Freezer = −18 °C
Coldest day = −5 °C
Hottest day = 35 °C
Fridge (Level 2) = 4 °C
Number-line reasoning: Numbers farther left are smaller (colder). Place each value on a mental number line:

  −18  <  −5  <  4  <  18  <  35

Sorted coldest to warmest:
−18, −5, 4, 18, 35 °C

Coldest: −18 °C  |  Warmest: 35 °C

Level 2 — range: 35 − (−18) = 53 °C of spread. Outlier detective: no single value sits far from the rest, so the readings are fairly evenly spread.
Ordered: −18, −5, 4, 18, 35 °C · range 53

Quick Check (Self · fixed answer) 6.NS.7c · Absolute Value

Quick Check: What is |−45|?
−45 is 45 units to the left of 0 on the number line.
|−45| = 45. Absolute value removes the sign; distance is always positive.
Correct answer: 45

JavaScript check: PK.checkWork("check-abs", 45, 0)

Part 2 — Peer Compare (worked example) 6.NS.7 · Compare & reason

Example: the student's depth reading is −120 m (|−120| = 120). Suppose a partner reports a lowest depth of −340 m, coldest temp −12 °C, warmest temp 29 °C.

|Depth| from sea level: me = |−120| = 120, partner = |−340| = 340 → difference = |340 − 120| = 220. The partner's lowest place is farther from sea level (deeper).

Merged temperatures (student fallback set −18, −5, 4, 18, 35 plus partner's −12 and 29), coldest → warmest:
  −18, −12, −5, 4, 18, 29, 35 °C
Combined coldest = −18 °C · combined warmest = 35 °C.
|Depth| 120 vs 340 (diff 220)  |  merged: −18 … 35 °C

Sample Expert Response (4/4 Deliverable)

Sample Mission Log using the "example numbers" set (student work will use their own real numbers):
"Mission log: My lowest place is −85 meters, meaning 85 meters below sea level — a negative integer because it is below the zero reference point; my highest is +2500 m above sea level. The absolute value |−120| = 120 tells the distance of my depth reading from sea level without the direction sign. My temperatures ordered from coldest to warmest are −18, −5, 4, 18, 35 °C; the most negative value is coldest because it sits farthest left on the number line. Compared with the world sample, my −120 m is much shallower than the −3688 m average ocean depth, because everyday places are close to sea level."
Sample 4 / 3 / 2 Rubric Scoring Guide:
4 — Expert: High/low correctly signed with real-world explanation; |n| stated and connected to "distance from zero"; all temperatures ordered correctly with number-line reasoning; peer and world comparisons justified with the absolute difference.
3 — Proficient: Correct signs, correct absolute value, correct ordering; comparisons present; log uses actual numbers but may skip some reasoning language.
2 — Developing: One or two values signed incorrectly; ordering has one transposition error; absolute value computed but not clearly connected to distance from zero.
