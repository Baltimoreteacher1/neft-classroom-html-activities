Theme Park Engineer: Area Architect 🎢

Lead engineer Maya has thirty days to deliver the most ambitious amusement park ever drafted. Every ride, plaza, and pavilion stays locked in the simulator until its surface area is calculated to the square meter. Master the area of parallelograms, triangles, and trapezoids — then fuse them into composite figures — to bring the whole park online.

Act 1 · Area of Parallelograms

The Slanted Plaza

🏗️ Build #1 — The Welcome Plaza

The entry plaza is a parallelogram. Use A = b × h, where h is the perpendicular height — not the slanted side. The base is 14 m, the perpendicular height is 6 m, and the slanted edge measures 7 m. What is the area?

Figure: Parallelogram ▱
base (b) = 14 m
perpendicular height (h) = 6 m — use THIS in the formula
slanted edge = 7 m — a trap; do NOT use it as the height

🏗️ Build #2 — The Reverse-Engineered Walkway

The walkway must cover exactly 96 m² of ground, and the blueprint fixes the base at 12 m. Working backward from A = b × h, what height must the parallelogram have?

Act 2 · Triangles & Trapezoids

Signs & Stages

🔥 Build #3 — The Beacon Sign

The triangular beacon sign uses A = ½ × b × h. The base spans 16 m and the height rises 9 m. Find the area of the sign face.

Figure: Triangle △
base (b) = 16 m
height (h) = 9 m — straight up from the base

🎪 Build #4 — The Trapezoid Canopy

The amphitheater canopy is a trapezoid: A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h. The parallel edges are 8 m and 14 m, and the height between them is 10 m. Compute the canopy area.

Figure: Trapezoid ⏢
top parallel side (b₁) = 8 m
bottom parallel side (b₂) = 14 m
height (h) = 10 m — distance between the two parallel sides
