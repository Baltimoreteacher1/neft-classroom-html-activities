Unit 8 HyperDoc — Statistics

Data Lab: build a plot, then read the mean, median, mode, and range. Follow the 5 steps from top to bottom.

Neft Teacher · Grade 6 Mathematics

Learning Target

  • I can recognize a statistical question and explain why it has variability. (6.SP.A.1)
  • I can find the mean, median, mode, and range of a data set. (6.SP.B.5)
  • I can interpret data displays (dot plots, histograms) and describe shape, center, and spread. (6.SP.A.2, 6.SP.B.4)
  • I can explain when the mean or median better describes a data set. (6.SP.A.3)
📐 Standards: 6.SP.A.1–3 · 6.SP.B.4–5 ⏱ Estimated time: 55 minutes 📦 Materials: pencil, this HyperDoc, optional dot-plot handout 👥 Format: individual or pair work

Teacher Notes (not for student display)

Pacing

  • Engage — 5 min: collect real sleep data from the class (show of hands); chart range on board.
  • Explore — 10 min: 3D Data Lab game + 3 min debrief on what measure changed most.
  • Explain — 8 min: work through the table example whole-class; students copy steps.
  • Apply — 5 min self-check + 15 min graded NTKit questions.
  • Reflect — 7 min individual writing; collect as PDF deliverable.

Grouping

  • Pair students for Explore; individual for Apply and Reflect.
  • For small groups: have students compare mean vs. median answers before checking.

Differentiation — Support

  • Provide a step-by-step "How to find the mean" reference card.
  • Allow calculator use for mean computation.
  • Reduce Apply to Q1–Q4 for students who need scaffolding.

Differentiation — Challenge

  • Ask: "If you add one very large number to a data set, which measures change? By how much?"
  • Extension: introduce Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD) as a measure of spread.
  • Have students create their own statistical question, collect data from the class, and compute all four measures.

ESOL / Language Supports

  • Post bilingual vocabulary: media/mean, mediana/median, moda/mode, rango/range, pregunta estadística/statistical question.
  • Use the sorted data table as a visual organizer — point to each step.
  • Accept numeric answers without full sentences for emerging English learners.
  • Sentence frames for reflection: "The median is better when … because …"
Step 1 · Engage

A Question Worth Measuring

Your class asks: "How many hours do we sleep each night?" Some students sleep 6 hours. Some sleep 10. The answers are different each time. That makes it a statistical question — it has many answers that vary.

Do this: Think of one question about your class that would get many different answers. You will write it in Step 5.

Non-statistical example: "How old am I?" — only one answer. Statistical example: "How old are the students in my class?" — many different answers.

Step 3 · Explain

The Words You Need

Mean — the average. Add all numbers, then divide by how many.
Median — the middle number when sorted small to big.
Mode — the number that shows up most.
Range — biggest number minus smallest number.
Statistical question — a question with answers that vary (not just one answer).
Distribution — the shape of data: symmetric (even), skewed (pulled to one side), or clustered.

Worked example. Hours of sleep: 6, 8, 8, 7, 6

First sort them: 6, 6, 7, 8, 8

MeasureHowAnswer
Mean(6+6+7+8+8) ÷ 5 = 35 ÷ 57
Medianmiddle of 6, 6, 7, 8, 87
Mode6 and 8 each appear twice6 and 8
Range8 − 62
Key idea: When there is an outlier (a very large or very small number), the mean gets pulled toward it. The median is usually a better description of "typical" in that case.
